About the Course

The Natural Language Processing (21CSE356T) course introduces core theories and practical methodologies to analyze and build systems capable of understanding human language. Bridging computing and linguistics, it systematically covers fundamental to advanced NLP techniques.

Learning Objectives by Unit

Unit 1 - Overview and Word Level Analysis
  • Understand the basics of NLP, key applications, and components of NLP pipelines.
  • Familiarity with regular expressions, morphological analysis, tokenization, stemming, and lemmatization.
  • Apply text preprocessing techniques including TF, IDF, TF-IDF, parts of speech tagging, N-gram models, and smoothing.
Unit 2 - Syntax Analysis
  • Comprehend context-free grammars and syntactic parsing for English.
  • Perform top-down and bottom-up parsing, including CKY and Earley algorithms.
  • Understand and apply probabilistic context-free grammars to analyze sentences.
Unit 3 - Semantic and Discourse Analysis
  • Represent meaning using lexical semantics, word senses, and relations.
  • Apply techniques like word embeddings, Word2Vec, CBOW, Skip-gram, and GloVe.
  • Analyze discourse coherence, structure, coreference resolution, and perform tasks like named entity recognition and semantic segmentation.
Unit 4 - Language Models
  • Understand and develop recurrent neural networks (RNN), LSTM, and transformer-based models for NLP.
  • Apply attention mechanisms and multi-headed attention in deep learning for text.
  • Execute text classification, text generation, and related tasks using advanced models like BERT and RoBERTa.
Unit 5 - NLP Applications
  • Develop solutions for chatbot applications and information retrieval.
  • Implement systems for conversation, intent detection, and semantic search.
  • Perform summarization, question answering, and machine translation using extractive and abstractive approaches.

Reflection

  1. What new skills did I gain?

    Acquired expertise in text preprocessing, such as stemming, lemmatization, and syntactic parsing, plus practical knowledge in feature extraction like TF, IDF, and TF-IDF. Learned how to design and analyze context-free grammars, implement semantic and discourse analysis with word embeddings, and build modern neural networks including RNNs, LSTMs, and transformer-based models for NLP. Gained experience in developing and deploying projects for tasks like chatbots, summarization, and information retrieval.

  2. What was most challenging?

    Mastering advanced parsing algorithms (CKY, Earley) and managing the complexity of semantic and discourse analysis proved difficult. Balancing fairness, interpretability, and performance while tuning large language models required deep experimentation. Applying transformer architectures effectively to diverse tasks, while controlling for data bias and model overfitting, was especially challenging given the breadth of real-world applications.

  3. How did I improve from start to end?

    Progressed from foundational rule-based approaches and classical ML models to mastering neural architectures and transfer learning. Enhanced my ability to prepare and clean data, select suitable features, interpret complex output from neural networks, and optimize models for deployment in real-world scenarios. Collaborative projects and iterative experimentation further strengthened both technical and soft skills throughout the semester.

  4. How can I apply this knowledge?

    This NLP knowledge can be applied to building advanced chatbots for customer support, developing intelligent information retrieval and search systems, summarizing complex documents, and enabling multilingual translation tools. It prepares me to tackle real challenges in healthcare (analyzing medical records), business intelligence, and education (automated feedback and grading), and empowers lifelong learning in AI and language technologies.
